October 2, 2020 by Kyle Campbell

Una Marson by Lisa Tomlinson is centered on the life of Una, born to Reverend Solomon Isaac and Ada Marson in Sharon Village, St. Elizabeth. Una, in her adult years, had amassed countless accolades under her belt and played parts in numerous movements such as anti-racism and feminist movements as well as made contributions in the political happenings, although such accolades were not typically associated with women, highlighting the pioneering roles that Una had put in motion. Lisa Tomlinson is a lecturer at the University or the West Indies, Jamaica, lecturing in the department of Literacy and Cultural Studies. Una Marson is one of the books that is a part of the Caribbean Biography series. Likewise, the literature will be discussed and analyzed in both a historical and social context to bring about an increased and improved understanding.

Lastly, the literature possessed valuable merit as it pertains to the course Caribbean Civilization, where the importance of an identity was highlighted. t was one of the first instances in which the reader had observed a deviation from what was expected of a woman and highlighted the will and passion that Una had possessed in her plight to empower woman, using herself and her actions as a form of inspiration and motivation.

Legacy

October 2, 2020 by Kyle Campbell

Colonization is the process by which a larger dominant territory or country exerts its dominance over a smaller, weaker, less-developed territory taking control of essential sectors such as the social, political and economic affairs. Colonization typically seeps into the societies of the smaller territories where the influence and customs of the larger territory, known as colonial influence, becomes embedded and entrenched in to the social, political and economic happenings of the smaller society. Colonization was an instrument of oppression and suppression aimed at eliminating Caribbean uniqueness and individuality and promotes European admiration. Based on the literature, the sector in which colonization was most notably observed in was that of the educational sector. The education system was developed and established in such a way as to promote European superiority and encourage European admiration. The education system was a vehicle in which Caribbean students could be subliminally and subconscious fed solely European content in an official setting that was taught to be ‘right’. By doing this, the European content and customs would be preserved and maintain in the society and would be an endless cycle of loyalty to the European way from generation to generation.
